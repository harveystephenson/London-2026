London 2026 — The Underground, Deep Dive

Two more serious attempts at a real tube-map grammar

The Underground concept from Vol. 2 tested well — this pushes it closer to how an actual Beck-style diagram is actually built: strict 45°/90° routing, small solid-colour stations vs. bullseye interchanges vs. capped termini, a key box in the corner, a hint of the Thames. It still can't map onto the real Piccadilly/Central network — Cambridge and the Cotswolds aren't on it — so both versions invent their own line names and geography-free routing, the way the real diagram invents its own geometry too.

Version 1
Refined Network

Same three strands as the Vol. 2 sketch — one Home Line, two branches — but drawn with real diagram grammar: small filled stations, bullseye interchanges at the two junctions, capped termini at both ends, a dashed "Greater London" boundary the branches visibly leave, and a pale Thames band under the Home Line. Busier in craft, not in structure.

Version 2
Full Network

The London home-base days split into two crossing lines instead of one — a City Line and a Riverside Line that share two stations (Heathrow and a "West End" interchange), the way the real Central and Circle lines cross more than once. The two day-trip branches still peel off the City Line. Four colours, three interchanges, a proper key — this is the one that actually looks crowded the way Zone 1 does.

Lines: 2 trunk lines (crossing twice) + 2 branches Trade-off: the City/Riverside split is a layout device, not a real geographic distinction — reads as busier, not more accurate

Weighing the Two
Which One

Refined Network is closer to what the site could actually ship: one line is enough information to build and maintain, the zone ring and river band do a lot of the "feels like a real tube map" work cheaply, and it stays legible at a glance.

Full Network looks the most like the real thing — genuinely busy, two lines crossing twice the way the Circle and District do — but the City/Riverside split doesn't correspond to anything real about the trip; it exists to create visual density. Worth it if the goal is "look like the Tube map," a needless complication if the goal is "represent this specific trip."

Either can carry the same interaction model: click a station to open that location's photo grid, same as the live map's flag pins do today.
