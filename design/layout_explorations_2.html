01b · Cartographic, Extended
The Route — Whole Trip

Yes — a long timeline, on purpose. All ten days on one continuous spine: light days collapse to a single compact stop, the two day-trip branches (Cotswolds, Cambridge) blow out into their full halt-by-halt detail, and the Rania dinner sits below a visibly broken connector, since it isn't really on this line at all. The page gets long; a slim sticky rail of ten ticks along the right edge tracks where you are in the trip as you scroll, so length doesn't mean getting lost.

Length: roughly 3–4x a single day's scroll depth Answers: "where am I in the trip" via the sticky rail, not a nav bar

01c · Cartographic — Route sibling
The Underground

Same skeleton as The Route — a spine with branches — redrawn as a schematic transit diagram instead of a straight vertical line. A "Home Line" runs London's own days left to right; two colored branches peel off at 45° for the Cotswolds and Cambridge day trips, the way a real Tube branch splits from a trunk line. Rania sits off in a corner, deliberately not connected to any line — which happens to match the real reason it has no map pin.

Best for: a trip with clear day-trip "branches," like this one Costs: station spacing has to be hand-tuned; doesn't scale gracefully past ~3 branches

02b · Editorial — Field Notes sibling
Postcards

Same idea as Field Notes — a day is a written, designed thing, not a table row — pushed into a specific object: the postcard you'd actually send home. Front side is a photo with a mock stamp and postmark; flip it and the back has a short handwritten-feeling note on the left, an address-line grid on the right (repurposed to hold the day's photo/stop counts), the way a real postcard splits.

Best for: single strong-photo days, browsed as a set Costs: a flip interaction to implement; nine postcards is a lot of DOM for one page

02c · Editorial — Field Notes sibling
Exhibition Placard

Field Notes as a museum wall text instead of a magazine page: a plate number, a title, a "medium and dimensions" line repurposed for stop/photo counts, a curatorial paragraph, and a small numbered grid of "exhibited plates" below — each photo captioned like a catalog object rather than a caption under a picture.

Best for: a formal, retrospective tone — this is a trip being exhibited, not narrated Costs: the driest of the four; needs real restraint to not feel cold for a family audience

Further Ideas, Not Yet Mocked Up

More variations on the two structures above — a linear/branching backbone (like The Route and The Underground) or a written, art-directed day (like Field Notes and Postcards). Say which one to build next.

Route Family — Linear / Branching Backbone
Ticket ChainEach day is a torn boarding-pass stub, chained edge to edge by a perforated line; branch days get a stapled second stub for the day trip.
River & ThreadThe straight spine becomes a hand-drawn, winding river or embroidery thread; days sit as beads along the curve instead of stations on a line — warmer, more illustrated, less technical than The Underground.
Flight PathA dark night-sky arc (great pairing with Night Reel from Vol. 1) with each day as a lit waypoint along a great-circle route; branch days flare off as short side-arcs.
Field Notes Family — Written, Art-Directed Day
BroadsheetA full newspaper front page: masthead, multi-column text wrap, a "classifieds" box for Confirmed Bookings instead of a card.
Travel DossierA vintage travel-agency ticket wallet — a die-cut folder holding "tickets" and a folded itinerary insert per day, opened like a booklet.
Zine / CollageCut-paper torn edges, mixed type sizes and angles, photocopier-style high-contrast photo treatment — the most DIY and least formal of any direction so far.
