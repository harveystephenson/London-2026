London 2026 — Structural Studies

Four ways to lay the trip out that aren't a stack of cards

The live site's palette work (four themes, one grid) is done. This is a different question: what if the shape of the page changed, not just its colors? Same information throughout — hero, day-by-day, map, photos, bookings — reorganized around four different structural ideas.

Every "photo" below is a placeholder gradient with a caption, not a real image — these are shape studies, not content-ready pages. The Cotswolds day (3 Jul, eight stops plus lunch) is used as the stress test throughout, since it's the busiest day on the itinerary.

01 · Cartographic
The Route

The itinerary becomes a transit line: one vertical spine running the length of the trip, each day a brass station stop. Busy days — like the Cotswolds — branch off the main line into their own local route with every stop as a halt. The map stops being a separate section and becomes the entire skeleton of the page.

02 · Editorial
Field Notes

Each day reads like a page torn from a travel magazine, not a database row: an oversized ghost numeral, a real headline instead of a schedule label, a dropped cap, a pull-quote lifted from the day. Photos run as an asymmetric masonry rather than a uniform grid — some stops earn a big frame, some a small one, the way an editor would actually lay out a spread.

Replaces: the schedule table with headline + running text + margin facts Keeps: stop list (moved to the margin), photo links, booking-style facts

Nine Villages Before Dinner

Out of London before eight, the coach climbed out of the M40 haze and into a different country entirely — one built from honey-colored stone and hedgerows. Broadway Tower came first, caught in a tight sixteen-minute cluster of photographs at the top of the hill.

"Every village looked like the last, and somehow that was the whole point."

Lunch broke the day at The Feathered Nest Inn, tucked into Nether Westcote — a two-hour pause between Lower Slaughter's single quiet photo and the long golden light of Bourton-on-the-Water, where the cameras came out thirty-eight times before anyone noticed the hour.

03 · Analog / Scrapbook
Corkboard

The whole day gets pinned to a corkboard like the trip planning wall it probably started as: tilted polaroids taped down at odd angles, a torn-paper map scrap with the route inked in red string, and the day's facts on a typewritten index card. Nothing lines up to a grid on purpose — it should feel handled, not laid out.

Replaces: the day block entirely with a pinboard scene Keeps: stop order (via the string route), photo count, location names

04 · Cinematic
Night Reel

Each day is its own full-bleed title card, like a documentary chapter — a timecode, a reel number, a big cropped headline, and a filmstrip of that day's shots underneath. A sprocket-holed reel counter runs down the right edge as the only persistent navigation: ten dots, one lit at a time, the whole trip visible as a single strip of film.

Replaces: the collapsible day list with one full-viewport chapter per day Keeps: stop order (as a chaptered run-list), photo emphasis, day-to-day navigation

Weighing the Four
Comparison

None of these are drop-in replacements for the live grid — each is a real rebuild of index.html's structure, not a CSS tweak. This is a rough sense of what each one costs and what it's actually good at.

ConceptBest atCosts youMobile
The Route Making the whole trip's shape scannable in one scroll; busy days branch instead of overwhelming. The spine has to be hand-tuned so branch days don't dwarf light ones. Naturally narrow — collapses well.
Field Notes Photo storytelling; days with a strong narrative arc (Cotswolds, Cambridge) shine. Masonry + margin column is the most layout work of the four; quieter days may feel over-written. Two-column spread has to fully stack; drop cap/pull-quote need simplifying.
Corkboard Warmth and personality — feels handmade, matches a family retrospective tone. Absolute-positioned scatter is the hardest to make responsive and accessible; needs a real reflow strategy per breakpoint. Hardest of the four — the "scattered" layout likely becomes a simple stack on phones, losing the effect.
Night Reel Drama and pacing for a slideshow-style read-through; strong on a big screen. Full-viewport chapters make skimming (the current "At a Glance" habit) slower — needs the reel rail to double as a jump-nav. Sprocket chrome likely drops; still works as stacked full-bleed sections.
Structural mockups only — no photo pipeline, theme variables, or build script changes implied. Say the word on any direction (or a hybrid) and it can move to a real day_layout_mockup.html-style pass against actual site data.
